The greenest product is almost always one that already exists. Manufacturing anything — clothing, furniture, electronics, toys — requires raw materials, energy, water, and generates emissions. When you buy something secondhand, all of that has already happened. You're extending the useful life of an object rather than triggering the production of a new one.

For families, this is also one of the most practical eco choices because children's items in particular are used for short periods and often remain in excellent condition. A 4-year-old outgrows a jacket in a season. A toddler toy is abandoned within months. The secondhand market for children's goods is large, cheap, and mostly excellent.

## Where secondhand is always the better choice

### Children's clothing

Kids grow so fast that most secondhand children's clothing has barely been worn. A school-age child might fit the same size for 9–12 months before needing the next. Buying new for that period is genuinely wasteful when excellent secondhand alternatives exist at a fraction of the price.

Charity shops, ThredUp, Facebook Marketplace, and local parent groups on WhatsApp or NextDoor are the main routes. For school uniforms specifically, many schools now run their own swap schemes — worth asking about if yours doesn't.

### Baby equipment

Bouncers, high chairs, baby gyms, nursing pillows, breast pumps — this equipment is expensive, used for months, and then sits in a loft forever. The secondhand market is enormous. The main exception is car seats (see below).

### Books

New books require paper, printing, and shipping. Secondhand books are identical in every way that matters. Libraries are free. Charity shops sell paperbacks for 50p. ThriftBooks and AbeBooks sell millions of used books online. There is almost no reason to buy most books new.

### Furniture

Solid wood furniture improves with age and lasts generations. Fast furniture made from compressed wood and laminate often can't be repaired and ends up in landfill within a few years. A solid oak dining table found secondhand for £80 is a better environmental and financial choice than a flat-pack equivalent at three times the price.

Facebook Marketplace, Gumtree, and local charity furniture shops are the main routes. Freecycle and local Facebook community groups often have free items for collection.

### Sports equipment

Children's bikes, skis, hockey gear, football boots — all grow out of quickly. Most are available in excellent condition secondhand for 20–30% of the new price. Sports-specific resale sites exist for most activities.

### Toys

Plastic toys are among the most resource-intensive items relative to their lifespan. Buying secondhand breaks that cycle. The caveat: check for recalls on older toys, and wash fabric toys before use.

## Where new is the right call

Buying secondhand isn't always the better choice. There are specific categories where new makes sense for safety, hygiene, or practical reasons.

### Car seats

This is the most important exception. Car seats have expiry dates (typically 6–10 years from manufacture), and secondhand seats may have been in accidents that compromised their structural integrity without any visible damage. Unless you can verify the full history of a car seat from someone you trust completely, buy new.

### Mattresses

Hygiene and structural integrity. A mattress that's been used for years may harbour allergens and have lost its support. New is the right call here — and buying a quality mattress that lasts 10+ years is the sustainable choice.

### Helmets

Like car seats, helmets may have absorbed impacts that compromise their protection without visible signs. Buy new.

### Underwear and swimwear

Hygiene.

### Safety-critical items

Any secondhand item with a safety function — stair gates, window locks, cot rails — should only be bought if you can verify it meets current safety standards and has no damage.

## Making secondhand easier as a habit

The main barrier to buying secondhand is time. Browsing charity shops or online listings takes more effort than clicking "buy now." A few approaches that make it easier:

**Search before you shop.** Before buying anything over $20 new, spend 5 minutes on Facebook Marketplace or a charity shop site first. This takes discipline to start but becomes automatic.

**Use dedicated platforms.** ThredUp for clothing, eBay for electronics, Vinted for fashion, AbeBooks for books, Facebook Marketplace for furniture and equipment. Each platform has strong inventory in its category.

**Set saved searches.** On most secondhand platforms you can save a search and receive alerts when matching items are listed. Set one up for items you know you'll need — the next size up of school shoes, a specific Lego set, a particular book.

**Join local parent groups.** A WhatsApp group of local parents is often the fastest source of good secondhand children's items, because people are giving away things their children just grew out of.

**Be realistic about condition.** "Good used condition" is fine for most things. Save your energy for inspecting anything where condition truly matters — furniture joints, electronics, fabric items for wear.

The shift to buying secondhand first doesn't require sacrifice. It requires a small change in the order of how you shop. Start with what you need, search secondhand first, buy new only when necessary. That sequence change alone makes a meaningful difference over a year of family spending.
