Children's clothing sits at the worst intersection of the fashion industry's problems. Kids outgrow clothes in 6–12 months. Quality at the affordable end of the market has declined sharply, so clothes often don't survive long enough to be passed on. And because it's children's clothing — small, cheap, and bought constantly — parents often don't think twice about the volume they're cycling through.

The result: the average child in the UK goes through around 70 new items of clothing per year. Most of it ends up in landfill within 24 months.

This is worth fixing — not just for environmental reasons, but because the financial case is equally strong. A family that changes how it buys children's clothes can easily save £300–500 per year without any reduction in what the children wear.

## Why children's clothing is fashion's biggest waste problem

Adult fashion gets most of the criticism — documentaries about sweatshops, campaigns against fast fashion brands, debates about capsule wardrobes. Children's clothing escapes scrutiny largely because individual items are cheap and seem inconsequential.

But the volume is the problem. A child moves through roughly 5–7 clothing sizes in their first decade. At each size, a full wardrobe is needed — school clothes, play clothes, smart occasion wear, sportswear, nightwear, underwear. Then the child grows out of it in months and it starts again.

The fibres in most cheap children's clothing — polyester blends, acrylic, nylon — are plastic-based. They don't biodegrade. A polyester school jumper bought for £4 and worn for one school year will still exist in a landfill in 200 years.

## The hand-me-down system: the highest-impact change

If there is one thing a family can do that makes the biggest difference, it's building a functioning hand-me-down system — either within the family or with trusted friends and neighbours.

This requires almost no additional cost or effort, just a small shift in habit:

**Store outgrown clothes by size.** As soon as something no longer fits, wash it, fold it, and put it in a labelled bag or box — "Age 3–4," "Age 5–6," and so on. Clear vacuum bags work well for space saving.

**Find a hand-me-down partner.** Another family with a child one or two sizes behind yours is the ideal arrangement. You pass clothes down, they eventually pass theirs to someone else. A WhatsApp group of local parents often makes this easy to arrange.

**Be honest about condition.** If something is genuinely worn out — pilled, stained, damaged — let it go. The goal is passing on clothes that still have useful life, not creating a social obligation to accept worn-out items.

A well-run hand-me-down system can cover 40–60% of a child's wardrobe at zero cost.

## Secondhand first: what the market looks like now

The secondhand children's clothing market has expanded significantly. The quality and selection available online now is genuinely excellent, and prices are a fraction of new.

**For everyday clothing:** ThredUp, Vinted, and Facebook Marketplace have enormous selections searchable by size and brand. eBay is particularly good for specific brands or items.

**For school uniform:** Many schools now run their own nearly-new uniform sales, usually at the start of the school year. If yours doesn't, suggest it — it's easy to organise and popular with parents.

**For special occasions:** Children wear occasion wear — christenings, weddings, school discos — once or twice at most. Buying secondhand for these events is obvious once you think about it. A dress worn twice and then sold on is a perfectly sensible outcome.

**For charity shops:** Physical charity shops are worth browsing for younger ages especially. Baby and toddler clothes turn up in excellent condition constantly because children grow out of them so fast.

## When to buy new — and what to look for

Some things are worth buying new: underwear, swimwear, and anything where fit and condition genuinely matters. When you do buy new, a few principles make a real difference:

**Choose natural fibres where possible.** Cotton, wool, and linen biodegrade. Polyester doesn't. For basics — t-shirts, leggings, underwear — organic cotton from brands like Pact or Frugi costs more per item but lasts longer, washes better, and doesn't shed microplastics.

**Buy one size up.** For most items except very fitted things, buying a size larger extends the wearable life by 6–12 months. Children's clothing with adjustable waistbands is especially useful here.

**Prioritise quality over quantity.** Five well-made t-shirts that survive a year of washing are better than ten cheap ones that pill and fade after three months. This is where the economics of quality start to make sense.

**Avoid licensed character clothing.** Peppa Pig and dinosaur-print clothes are fashion items — they go out of favour as the child moves on, reducing their secondhand value and making them harder to pass on. Plain or simply patterned clothes have longer useful lives and are easier to sell or give away.

## What to do with outgrown clothes

Even with the best system, some children's clothing will reach the end of its useful life. Options in rough order of preference:

1. **Pass on within your network** — still wearable but your child has outgrown it
2. **Sell online** — Vinted, Facebook Marketplace, or school swap schemes
3. **Donate to charity** — only if in genuinely good condition; charity shops bin low-quality donations
4. **Textile recycling banks** — worn-out clothing can go here rather than landfill; H&M and many supermarkets have collection points
5. **Landfill** — the last resort, for genuinely unusable items

The one thing to avoid: binning clothes that still have life in them. Even heavily worn children's clothing has value as fabric recycling or rags. The textile bank is always a better outcome than the bin.

## The realistic target

You don't need a perfect zero-waste wardrobe. A practical, realistic target for most families:

- 50% secondhand or hand-me-down
- 30% quality new items in natural fibres, bought to last
- 20% flexible — whatever makes sense at the time

That shift alone dramatically reduces both the financial and environmental cost of dressing children. And it removes the guilt that comes from watching a perfectly good piece of clothing get outgrown in four months.
