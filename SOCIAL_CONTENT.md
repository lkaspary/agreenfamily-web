### Thread 3 — Fast Fashion Kids
*Article: /articles/fast-fashion-kids*

**Tweet 1 (hook):**
The average child goes through 70 new items of clothing per year. Most of it is synthetic fibre that won't biodegrade for 200 years. I didn't think about this until I added up what we were spending. 🧵

**Tweet 2:**
My two kids used to go through clothes like it was a competitive sport. A shirt would look fine in the store and be threadbare by Christmas. We were buying constantly and it felt like it was never enough.

**Tweet 3:**
What changed: secondhand first, always. About 60–70% of what our kids wear now is secondhand or hand-me-downs. The quality is often better than new fast fashion — because secondhand self-selects for things that actually held up.

**Tweet 4:**
The lesson that took us longest: some things ARE worth paying more for new. A good winter coat lasts three seasons. Cheap school trousers last one. Pay once vs pay twice is real.

**Tweet 5:**
I come from a family where clothes were not thrown away. They were handed down, altered, repaired, and eventually cut into rags. My grandmother would have been genuinely confused by the idea of buying a child a new shirt every few months. Full guide → [link]

### Post 2 — Composting
*Pair with article image: family-composting.jpg*

My wife grew up in a house where food scraps went to the chickens, and what didn't go to the chickens went into a compost heap that fed the garden that fed the family.

Nobody called it composting. It was just what you did with the peels.

We lost that habit somewhere along the way — the way you lose habits when life gets more convenient. Then one weekend I looked at the bag of trash we were throwing out and realized it was almost entirely food scraps and packaging. Half of it didn't need to be garbage at all.

Getting back to composting was easier than I expected. And our younger kid, who takes the scraps outside to the bin every day, treats it like the most important job in the house.

Which, honestly, it kind of is.

### Post 3 — Glass vs Cans
*Pair with article image: glass-cans-plastic.jpg*

We stood in the supermarket aisle for an embarrassingly long time — one hand holding a glass jar of tomato passata, the other holding a can.

The glass felt more virtuous. The can felt more practical. We had no idea which one was actually better, and the packaging wasn't helping.

Here's what I found out: glass is heavy. A glass jar weighs four times more than the same amount in a can — which means more fuel burned to ship it from wherever it was made. The eco math isn't as clean as it looks.

But glass wins completely when you reuse the jar. We keep every decent one that comes through the house. They hold our dried beans, our spices, the sofrito that's always in the freezer.

And for dry goods? We buy loose-leaf tea in bulk and store it in metal tins — tins are actually better than glass for tea because they block light and seal the aroma in. (We use Full Leaf Tea Company. No affiliate link. They have no idea we exist.)
